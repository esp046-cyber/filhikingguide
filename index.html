üìã Pre-Hike Preparation
üèîÔ∏è Research Your Mountain
Study the trail difficulty, elevation gain, and terrain type. Read recent trip reports and reviews. Check if permits or guides are required. Know the estimated hiking time and plan accordingly.
üìù Register & Get Permits
Register with local authorities, DENR, or tourism office. Secure all necessary permits at least 1-2 weeks in advance. Hire accredited local guides (required for most major peaks). Respect indigenous communities and sacred sites.
üå¶Ô∏è Check Weather Forecast
Monitor weather 7 days before your hike. Check PAGASA website for updates. Be prepared to postpone if conditions are unfavorable. Never hike during typhoon warnings or heavy rain forecasts.
üí™ Physical Conditioning
Train at least 4-6 weeks before major hikes. Do cardio exercises (jogging, stair climbing, cycling). Practice with a loaded backpack. Build leg strength with squats and lunges. Don't skip rest days.
üì± Share Your Itinerary
Give detailed itinerary to family/friends. Include trailhead location, expected return date/time, and emergency contacts. Check in when you return safely. Consider leaving a copy with barangay officials.
ü•æ During The Hike
üö∂ Pace Yourself
Start slow and maintain a steady rhythm. Follow the "talk test" - you should be able to hold a conversation. Take short breaks every 45-60 minutes. Don't try to keep up with faster hikers if uncomfortable.
üíß Hydration Strategy
Drink 150-200ml water every 20-30 minutes. Don't wait until you're thirsty. Bring electrolyte powder for long hikes. Purify water from streams using tablets or filters. Avoid drinking directly from unknown water sources.
üç´ Eat Regularly
Snack every 1-2 hours to maintain energy. Bring high-calorie foods (nuts, energy bars, dried fruits). Eat a proper meal during longer breaks. Don't skip breakfast before starting. Pack extra food for emergencies.
üå°Ô∏è Layer Your Clothing
Start with fewer layers and add as needed. Remove layers before you start sweating. Base layer should be moisture-wicking, not cotton. Keep rain gear accessible. Bring extra warm clothes for summit attempts.
üë£ Proper Foot Care
Wear broken-in boots (never new shoes). Use proper hiking socks (avoid cotton). Tape hot spots immediately to prevent blisters. Keep feet dry when possible. Bring extra socks to change into.
üéí Pack Weight Distribution
Heavy items should be close to your back and centered. Keep frequently-used items easily accessible. Pack should not exceed 20% of body weight. Adjust straps regularly for comfort. Use hip belt to transfer weight from shoulders.
üó∫Ô∏è Navigation Tips
Stay on marked trails at all times. Use trail markers and cairns as guides. Check your map and compass regularly. Download offline maps before the hike. Mark your campsite location on GPS.
üë• Group Dynamics
Never hike alone - minimum 3 people recommended. Stay together and set a comfortable pace for all. Assign a sweep (last person) to ensure no one is left behind. Do regular headcounts. Help slower members with their packs if needed.
üèïÔ∏è Camping & Campsite
‚õ∫ Choose Campsite Wisely
Camp only in designated areas if available. Look for level, dry ground away from water sources (at least 60m). Avoid camping under dead trees or on ridges. Check for ant hills and wasp nests. Set up before dark.
üî• Campfire Safety
Check if campfires are allowed (many mountains ban them). Use existing fire rings when available. Keep fires small and controlled. Never leave fire unattended. Completely extinguish before sleeping or leaving. Use camping stove as safer alternative.
üêª Food Storage
Store food in sealed containers away from tent. Hang food bags on trees if animals are present. Never eat inside your tent. Clean up all food scraps immediately. Don't leave food or scented items in your tent overnight.
üåô Night Hiking Safety
Use headlamp with red light mode to preserve night vision. Always hike with at least one partner at night. Watch your footing carefully on uneven terrain. Bring backup lights and extra batteries. Avoid night hikes on unfamiliar trails.
üåø Environmental Responsibility
‚ôªÔ∏è Leave No Trace Principles
Pack out ALL trash including biodegradable waste. Toilet paper and sanitary items must be packed out. Don't bury trash - animals will dig it up. Leave what you find - no souvenirs. Take only photos, leave only footprints.
üöΩ Proper Waste Disposal
Dig catholes 15-20cm deep for human waste. Use toilet at least 60m from water sources. Pack out toilet paper in ziplock bags. Cover and disguise catholes after use. Use biodegradable soap sparingly.
üå∏ Respect Flora & Fauna
Don't pick flowers or collect plants. Stay on designated trails to avoid erosion. Don't feed or approach wildlife. Keep noise levels down. Observe animals from a distance. Respect bird nesting areas.
üèûÔ∏è Cultural Sensitivity
Respect sacred sites and indigenous customs. Ask permission before taking photos of locals. Follow guide's instructions about cultural practices. Don't enter restricted areas. Learn basic local greetings. Support local communities by hiring local guides.
‚ö†Ô∏è Safety & Emergency
üå©Ô∏è Weather Changes
Philippine mountain weather changes quickly. Watch for dark clouds and sudden wind changes. Seek shelter immediately if lightning threatens. Don't continue to summit if weather worsens. It's always okay to turn back for safety.
ü§ï Common Injuries Prevention
Prevent blisters with proper socks and boots. Treat hot spots immediately with tape or moleskin. Use trekking poles to reduce knee strain. Watch for slippery rocks and roots. Take care on descents (most injuries happen going down).
ü•µ Heat-Related Issues
Start early to avoid midday heat. Wear light-colored, breathable clothing. Use sun protection (hat, sunscreen, sunglasses). Watch for heat exhaustion symptoms (dizziness, nausea, excessive sweating). Rest in shade if overheated.
ü•∂ Cold & Altitude
Hypothermia can occur even in PH mountains above 2000m. Keep dry and wear proper layers. Watch for altitude sickness symptoms (headache, nausea, fatigue). Ascend slowly to acclimatize. Descend if symptoms worsen.
üêç Wildlife Encounters
Watch where you step and place your hands. Wear long pants in tall grass. Make noise while hiking to avoid surprises. Don't provoke or corner animals. Know basic first aid for snake bites. Most Philippine snakes avoid humans.
üìû Emergency Signals
Three of anything means distress (whistles, shouts, light flashes). Stay put if lost - make yourself visible. Use mirror or phone screen to signal aircraft. Create SOS sign with rocks or bright clothing. Conserve phone battery for emergencies.
üì∏ Photography Tips
üì∑ Capture The Moment
Golden hour (sunrise/sunset) provides best lighting. Bring extra batteries and memory cards. Protect camera from moisture with dry bags. Use burst mode for action shots. Don't sacrifice safety for photos. Respect "no photography" areas.
üáµüá≠ Filipino Hiking Etiquette
ü§ù Trail Courtesy
Greet fellow hikers with "Good morning/afternoon". Uphill hikers have right of way. Step aside to let faster hikers pass. Offer help if you see someone struggling. Share trail information about conditions ahead. Be friendly and respectful to everyone.
üéµ Noise Levels
Keep voices down to preserve nature's tranquility. Don't play loud music on trails. Respect others seeking peace in nature. Keep celebrations at campsites considerate. Be especially quiet during early morning hours.

üÜò Emergency Procedures
‚ö†Ô∏è General Emergency Response
1. STOP and assess the situation calmly
2. Ensure scene safety for yourself and others
3. Check for injuries - attend to most serious first
4. Call for help immediately (911 or local emergency)
5. Provide first aid if trained to do so
6. Keep the person warm and comfortable
7. Do NOT move someone with suspected spine injury
8. Document what happened and time of incident
üìç If Someone Gets Lost
1. Note last known location and time
2. Establish last seen wearing/carrying what
3. Call emergency services immediately
4. Stay in group - send 2 people max to search
5. Use whistles (3 blasts = distress signal)
6. Mark trails to avoid getting more people lost
7. If you're lost: STOP, stay put, signal for help
8. Make yourself visible with bright colors
ü§ï Injury Response Steps
1. Check for consciousness (talk to them)
2. Check breathing and pulse
3. Control bleeding with direct pressure
4. Immobilize suspected fractures
5. Treat for shock (lay flat, elevate legs, keep warm)
6. Never give unconscious person food/water
7. Monitor vital signs continuously
8. Prepare for evacuation if necessary
üè• First Aid Quick Guide
ü©π Cuts & Wounds
- Rinse with clean water to remove debris
- Apply direct pressure to stop bleeding
- Clean with antiseptic wipes
- Apply antibiotic ointment if available
- Cover with sterile bandage
- Watch for signs of infection (redness, swelling, pus)
- Change dressing daily or when wet
üë£ Blisters Treatment
- Do NOT pop unless absolutely necessary
- Clean area with alcohol or antiseptic
- If must drain: sterilize needle, drain from side
- Apply antibiotic ointment
- Cover with moleskin or blister bandage
- Prevention: tape hot spots immediately
- Keep feet dry and change socks regularly
ü¶¥ Sprains & Fractures
Sprains (RICE method):
- Rest the injured area
- Ice for 20 mins every 2 hours
- Compress with elastic bandage
- Elevate above heart level

Fractures:
- Don't move injured area
- Immobilize with splint
- Apply ice to reduce swelling
- Seek immediate medical help
üêç Snake Bite Response
- Stay calm and still (slows venom spread)
- Remove jewelry/tight clothing near bite
- Keep bitten area below heart level
- Do NOT cut wound or try to suck venom
- Do NOT apply ice or tourniquet
- Try to identify snake (from safe distance)
- Get to hospital immediately
- Most Philippine snakes are non-venomous
üêù Insect Stings
- Remove stinger by scraping (don't pinch)
- Wash area with soap and water
- Apply cold compress to reduce swelling
- Take antihistamine if available
- Watch for allergic reaction signs
- Seek help if difficulty breathing occurs
- Multiple stings require medical attention
ü•µ Heat Exhaustion
Symptoms: Heavy sweating, weakness, dizziness, nausea, headache

Treatment:
- Move to shade immediately
- Remove excess clothing
- Drink water slowly
- Apply cool wet cloths
- Rest completely
- If symptoms worsen ‚Üí Heat Stroke (call 911)
ü•∂ Hypothermia
Symptoms: Shivering, confusion, slurred speech, drowsiness

Treatment:
- Get to shelter immediately
- Remove wet clothing
- Wrap in blankets/sleeping bag
- Give warm (not hot) drinks
- Apply heat packs to core areas
- Seek medical help if severe
- Prevention: Stay dry and layered
‚õ∞Ô∏è Altitude Sickness (AMS)
Symptoms: Headache, nausea, fatigue, dizziness, difficulty sleeping

Treatment:
- Stop ascending immediately
- Rest and hydrate
- Take pain reliever for headache
- If symptoms persist: DESCEND
- Do not continue up if feeling worse
- Severe AMS requires immediate descent
- Prevention: Ascend slowly, stay hydrated
üí¶ Dehydration
Symptoms: Thirst, dark urine, fatigue, dizziness, dry mouth

Treatment:
- Drink water slowly and consistently
- Add electrolyte powder if available
- Rest in shade
- Avoid further exertion
- Eat salty snacks
- Prevention: Drink before you're thirsty
üå©Ô∏è Weather Emergencies
‚ö° Lightning Safety
If caught in storm:
- Seek low ground (not summit/ridge)
- Avoid tall trees and metal objects
- Get away from water
- Crouch low with feet together
- Don't lie flat on ground
- Wait 30 mins after last thunder before moving
- If someone is struck: start CPR if needed
üåßÔ∏è Flash Flood Response
- Never camp in dry riverbeds
- Move to higher ground immediately
- Don't attempt to cross flowing water
- If swept away: keep feet downstream, float on back
- Grab onto stable objects if possible
- 6 inches of moving water can knock you down
üì± Communication During Emergency
üìû What to Report
When calling emergency services, provide:
1. Your exact location (GPS coordinates if possible)
2. Number of people in your group
3. Nature and severity of emergency
4. Current condition of injured person
5. Your contact number
6. Landmarks or trail name
7. Weather conditions
8. Available resources (first aid, shelter)

üìù Emergency Action Plan
Before every hike, establish an Emergency Action Plan (EAP):
- Designate a group leader and decision maker
- Know location of nearest hospital/clinic
- Have emergency contact numbers saved offline
- Establish communication protocol
- Set check-in times with emergency contact
- Know evacuation routes from campsite
- Brief all members on emergency procedures
- Carry written copy of EAP
üè• Nearest Hospitals by Region
Before hiking, research nearest medical facilities to your mountain. Save their contact numbers. Know approximate distance and travel time. Have evacuation plan ready. Consider travel time from trailhead to hospital.
